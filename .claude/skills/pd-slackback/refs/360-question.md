## The Company 360 Question

> "Based on what you've observed of this person, which of their behaviors, skills, knowledge or expertise do you believe helps them to succeed? Provide specific examples and consider the company values."

This is the exact question your draft must answer. Every sentence in the review draft must be traceable to a named SBI block extracted in Step 6.

## What the Question Asks For

| Element | What it requires |
|---------|-----------------|
| "behaviors, skills, knowledge or expertise" | Name the specific capability demonstrated — not a general positive impression |
| "helps them to succeed" | Connect the behavior to an outcome — do not stop at describing what they did |
| "specific examples" | Name the project, date, channel, and the observable act — SBI blocks satisfy this requirement |
| "consider the company values" | Reference at least one named company value per example — use the exact value name |

## Draft Structure

Use the following structure for the 360 review draft. Adjust paragraph count based on evidence volume — do not pad with thin examples.

```
[Opening sentence: name the most distinctive capability observed]

[Body paragraph per major SBI cluster: 2–4 sentences each]
  - Name the situation (project, date range)
  - Describe the behavior (what they said or did, specific)
  - Name the impact (outcome, what changed)
  - Reference the company value(s) by name

[Closing sentence: forward-looking statement grounded in the pattern of evidence, not sentiment]
```

## Drafting Rules

1. **Ground every claim.** Each behavioral claim must map to a named SBI block. If a claim has no backing block, cut it.
2. **Name values explicitly.** Write "this demonstrates Own It" or "reflects Raise the Bar" — do not leave the value mapping implicit.
3. **No filler praise.** Phrases like "incredible collaborator", "always goes above and beyond", or "a true asset to the team" are not acceptable without a specific example immediately following.
4. **One example per paragraph.** Do not compress multiple distinct moments into one paragraph — reviewers cannot evaluate compound claims.
5. **Factual tone throughout.** Write as a professional statement of observed fact. Avoid emotional language, superlatives, or reference-letter framing.
6. **Closing sentence is pattern-based.** The closing sentence summarises the pattern across all evidence. It does not introduce new praise not supported by the SBI blocks.

## Worked Example

**Scenario**: Sarah Chen, Jan–Apr 2026. Evidence: P1 incident timeline post, schema migration review, cross-team unblocking on Beacon launch.

**Draft**:

Sarah demonstrates a consistent pattern of structured problem-solving under pressure that accelerates resolution for her team and adjacent teams.

During the February 2026 P1 database incident, Sarah posted a ranked-hypothesis incident timeline within 20 minutes of the alert, tagging the on-call team and providing three candidate root causes ordered by likelihood. The incident resolved 40 minutes faster than the team's historical average. This reflects Own It — she treated the incident as hers to drive without a formal assignment — and Dive Deep, evidenced by the structured hypothesis ranking rather than a single guess.

In March 2026, Sarah reviewed the Beacon launch integration spec in #beacon-launch and identified a missing API contract between two services before the launch date. Her catch prevented a production outage and was credited by the backend lead in the post-launch retro. This reflects Raise the Bar: she spotted a gap that others had passed over and escalated it with a proposed fix rather than a problem report alone.

Across these moments, Sarah's pattern is to move from problem recognition to structured action quickly, with her outputs typically unblocking others rather than requiring follow-up clarification. This is the behavior that makes her a reliable collaborator on high-stakes work.
